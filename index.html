The Owner's Rights

Owning a common stock isn't just a number in an account; it's a slice of ownership in a business. This ownership comes with a specific set of rights that give you influence and a stake in the company's future.

Common vs. Preferred Stock

Not all stocks are created equal. Companies can issue two main types: common and preferred. While both represent ownership, they offer very different features for investors. This section breaks down the key distinctions in rights, returns, and risk.

Who Gets Paid First? (Liquidation Priority)

In case of bankruptcy, there's a clear pecking order for who gets the company's remaining assets. This chart shows that common stockholders are last in line, which is a key reason for their higher risk and higher potential reward.

Two Sides of the Coin

Issuing and investing in stock involves a strategic exchange between companies seeking funds and investors seeking growth. This section explores both perspectives: why a company decides to "go public" and what benefits and risks an investor takes on.

Why Companies Issue Stock

For a company, issuing stock is a powerful way to raise money without taking on debt. This capital fuels growth, from building new factories to funding research. It's a trade-off: they get cash, but give up a portion of ownership.

    Why Investors Buy Stock

    For an investor, buying stock is a bet on a company's future success. It's a high-stakes game with unique risks and rewards, fundamentally different from the safety of a savings account.
